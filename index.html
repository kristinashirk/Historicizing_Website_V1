Historicizing Technology

Christina Haas presents a sociological perspective on the interaction between old and new technologies in the workplace. She cites the work of sociologists Vygotsky and Bijker as the basis for her claim that overly simple views of technology in the classroom may lead to a silencing of students who resist technology and a flattening of the different effects, both good and ill, that technology can have on individuals and groups. () Using three workplace examples of the intersection of new and old technologies, Haas demonstrates that:

  • (a) competing views of old and new technologies exist in workplaces,
  • (b) multiple literacy technologies are copresent in the conduct of work, and
  • (c) more advanced technologies are not necessarily the powerful technologies in work sites. ()

Indeed, Haas shows that new technologies do not simply replace new technologies, but rather are in a constant state of evolution. This means that older technologies will coexist with newer technologies in a workplace, much like the coexistence of homo erectus and homo sapiens in the evolution of apes. This makes sense. It is difficult to pull the rug on one way of doing things when it is entrenched in the workplace. So it would be beneficial and logical to slowly phase in new technologies alongside the old to aid in the transition.

The nav element is a perfect example of the coexistence of new and old technologies. The nav element was introduced in HTML 5, giving web designers a designated element to distinguish their navigation elements from other block-level elements in their site. The nav element is also used in accessibility tools like screen readers to distinguish navigation elements from the main page content. Traditionally, navigation elements were simply sequestered in a div element, and designers would use ARIA landmarks to signal the navigation function of the div to accessibility tools. The nav element simplified this process. But both of these practices still exist and are in use today. They are both valid methods of separating out navigation elements for web designers and marking the elements for accessibility purposes. This demonstrates Haas’ point that technology is simply evolutionary, and that old and new technologies commonly coexist.
